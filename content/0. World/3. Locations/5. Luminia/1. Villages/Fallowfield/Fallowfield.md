# History

## The Families
**Generation 1 (The Split)**: The Hendrik and Willem families divide, taking their farmhands and close friends with them

**Generation 2-3**: Marriage alliances form. Other prominent families pick sides based on business relationships, debts, and marriages. The mill owner sides with Eastern Fallowfield because Hendrik's son married his daughter. The blacksmith goes to Western Fallowfield because Willem helped him through a bad harvest.

**Generation 4-8**: The feud becomes institutionalized. Children are born into it. New families moving to the area must "declare" for one side or the other.

**Present Day (Generation 10+)**: The feud IS the village identity.

### The Eastenders
- **The Hendriksson Dynasty**: Still the "first family," they run the largest farm and the grain mill. Matriarch Constance Hendriksson chairs the Council of Grievances.
- **The Goldbrook Merchants**: Came for trade, stayed for the feud. They control river shipping and ensure Eastern Fallowfield pays "proper" docking fees.
- **The Ironwood Family**: Run the brewery and claim their "Upright Ale" recipe was stolen and corrupted into Eastern Fallowfield's "Lowland Lager."
- **The Weatherby-Finches**: The intellectual elite who maintain the Archive of Slights, a detailed record of every wrong committed by Eastern Fallowfield.

### The Westarters
- **The Willem's Rest Clan**: Willem's descendants who run the inn that "welcomed travelers when Western Fallowfield turned them away" (this was one guy, once, 150 years ago)
- **The Riverside Coopers**: Barrel-makers who insist Western Fallowfield's brewery would be nothing without their craftsmanship (conveniently forgetting they sell to both sides)
- **The Moonridge Family**: Control the fishing rights and ferry service, perpetually in a permit war with Western Fallowfield's dock masters
- **The Ashford-Greens**: Keep the Counter-Archive of Justified Responses, explaining why every action was actually retaliation

### "Neutral" Families
- **The Millhopper Family**: Literally split down the middle - they built houses on both sides and feud WITH THEMSELVES at family dinners
- **The Greywater Traders**: Officially neutral merchants who secretly fuel the feud by spreading gossip both ways
- **The Silverman Travelers**: A family of tinkers who change their scarves colors depending on which village they're in

## The Youth
Many young people find the feud exhausting but participate out of family pressure. Secret "peace meetings" happen at midnight, disguised as "reconnaissance missions." There's an entire underground network of cross-village friendships, romances, and a shared betting pool on which village will commit the pettiest act each month.

## Rules of the Feud
The feud has rules, passed down through sacred "Grudge Stones" ancient tablets that each village guards jealously. No one may cross except on market days. No intermarriage. No sharing of recipes. No helping during emergencies (though this one gets conveniently forgotten when actual disasters strike).

## Infrastructure of Spite
Both villages have developed parallel everything:
- Two different calendars (off by one day)
- Two "Original" recipes for the same feast dishes
- Competing libraries with the "true" history
- Separate market days (causing traveling merchants endless frustration)
- Competing "Fortune tellers" who predict opposite futures
- Different words for common items

## The Enablers / Benefactors
**The Council of Grievances** (East) and **The Committee of Justified Response** (West) are the official feuding bodies. They meet monthly to formally register complaints and plan retaliations. Members get a small stipend, good meals, and social prestige - they have a vested interest in keeping the feud going.

**The Feast of Grievances**: An annual event where each side performs plays about the other's wrongdoings. These have become elaborate productions that everyone secretly enjoys.

**The Merchants' Paradox**: Many families profit from the feud (selling different colored cloth, printing competing songbooks, brewing rival ales) but would never admit they don't want it to end.

## Persistence
- **Economic Investment**: Entire careers built on maintaining the feud (official scribes, grievance councils, competitive festivals)
- **Social Identity**: People define themselves by their village - ending the feud means existential crisis
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy**: "Our great-grandparents suffered for this feud, we can't just give up now!"
- **Entertainment Value**: Life in rural fantasyland is boring - the feud provides drama, purpose, and structure
- **Young Love**: The forbidden romance angle has created three generations of passionate marriages between villages (after elaborate fake "kidnappings" to save face)
